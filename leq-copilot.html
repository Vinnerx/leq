Overview

This resource presents the Learning Experience Questionnaire (LEQ) as a systematic, collegial and scientific approach to course evaluation and course development at Umeå University.

LEQ examines prerequisites for student learning by focusing on empirically grounded learning factors that research has shown to promote learning in higher education.

The guide supports teachers in analysing the learning environment of a course and using this analysis as a basis for course development. The appendices explain the theoretical structure of LEQ and include forms for course analysis and course reflection.

Why this matters: LEQ shifts attention from satisfaction or performance to conditions for learning, supporting development work grounded in research.

The LEQ Process

The LEQ process is an ongoing development cycle rather than an administrative routine.

1. New course offering
A course is completed. Formative evaluation during the course may already inform adjustments.
Purpose: Provides the concrete experience that initiates the cycle.
2. Course survey using LEQ
Students respond to the LEQ questionnaire at the end of the course.
Purpose: Collects systematic data on the learning environment.
3. Students’ course reflection (optional)
A group of students jointly interprets survey results and passes reflections to the teacher.
Purpose: Clarifies student perspectives and supports interpretation.
4. Course analysis
The teacher drafts a course analysis, discusses it collegially, and confirms a final version.
Purpose: Interprets data and identifies development priorities.
5. Competence development
Shared needs identified may lead to competence development activities.
Purpose: Strengthens teaching practice collectively.
6. Course development
Planned changes are implemented, often in the next course offering.
Purpose: Tests development ideas in practice.
Interpret with care: Each step connects to the next, forming a cycle of experience, reflection, analysis and development.

The Questionnaire

The LEQ questionnaire is built around 15 learning factors, operationalised through 23 statements. Statements 1–22 concern the learning environment and are grouped into three dimensions.

Meaningfulness (statements 1–7)

a) Meaningful content
Statements: 1
Learning is promoted when content is perceived as interesting, exciting or important.
b) Active trial
Statements: 2
Trying ideas and learning from experience supports deep learning.
c) Challenge and support
Statements: 3, 22
Learning benefits from environments that are both challenging and supportive.
d) Belonging and autonomy
Statements: 4, 5
A sense of community and trust in students’ ability to learn supports engagement.
e) Collaboration and peer learning
Statements: 6, 21
Learning with others facing similar challenges promotes understanding.
f) Practice and feedback
Statements: 7, 15
Opportunities to practice and receive feedback support learning before assessment.

Comprehensibility (statements 8–16)

g) Clear organisation
Statements: 8
Understanding how the course is organised supports orientation.
h) Prior knowledge and communication
Statements: 9, 17
Learning is supported when communication is clear and adapted to students’ prior knowledge.
i) Constructive alignment
Statements: 10, 11
Alignment between outcomes and activities supports understanding of learning goals.
j) Fair assessment
Statements: 12
Perceived fairness and clarity in assessment support trust and engagement.
k) Concrete examples
Statements: 13
Examples help students move from concrete experience to general principles.
l) Key concepts
Statements: 14
Focus on key concepts supports coherent understanding.
m) Reflective observation
Statements: 16
Reflection connects new experiences to prior knowledge.

Manageability (statements 17–22)

n) Enough time and effort
Statements: 18, 23
Learning requires sufficient time and student effort.
o) Variation and participation
Statements: 19, 20
Variation and opportunities to influence learning support manageability.
In practice: Learning factors link abstract theory to concrete aspects of the learning environment.

Polar Diagram Explainer

The polar diagram visualises students’ average responses to statements 1–22.

  • Points further from the centre indicate that more students considered the statement to be true.
  • A more circular shape suggests a more beneficial learning environment in terms of the learning factors.
  • Weaker aspects are not automatically problems to fix.
  • The diagram supports analysis and reflection, not normative judgement.
  • Statement 23 (own effort) is not included.

Practical Instructions

Purpose: Collect systematic student feedback on the learning environment.

Resources: Canvas LEQ template, UmU Play support videos.

  • Inform students early about why and how evaluation is conducted.
  • Report previous evaluation results and implemented changes.
  • Carry out the survey after the last examination.

Purpose: Allow students to jointly interpret survey results.

Resources: Survey&Report, UmU Play.

  • Students work independently without the teacher present.
  • Groups of 4–6 students are suitable.

Purpose: Analyse survey results and document conclusions.

Resources: Survey&Report.

  • Use qualitative content analysis for open questions.
  • Make the final analysis available to students.

Purpose: Collegial exchange of teaching experience.

  • Prepare a preliminary course analysis.
  • LEQ supports discussion rather than judgement.

Course Analysis Meeting

1. Pedagogical structure
Presentation of how each course is organised.
2. Learning environment
Stronger and weaker aspects based on the polar diagram.
3. Course development
Discussion of possible development and challenges.
4. Final reflection
Reflection on the meeting and its outcomes.
  • Different courses are usually preferable.
  • Participants prepare a preliminary course analysis.
  • The chairperson facilitates rather than presents.
  • The purpose is collegial exchange of concrete teaching experience.
  • LEQ is an aid to analysis, not a normative judgement tool.

Course Analysis Forms

Form for Course Analysis

This form supports systematic documentation of survey results, analysis of the learning environment, and prioritisation of course development.

  • Context and outcomes: Course data, response rate, and student achievement.
  • Learning environment: Analysis of statements 1–22 and own effort.
  • Student perspectives: Synthesis of open-question responses.
  • Development priorities: Planned short- and long-term improvements.

Form for Course Reflection

This form helps student groups interpret survey results and suggest course development priorities.

  • Learning environment: Joint interpretation of statements.
  • Open questions: Identification of important themes.
  • Suggestions: Motivated proposals for development.
