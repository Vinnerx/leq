Overview

This resource presents the Learning Experience Questionnaire (LEQ) as a systematic, collegial, and research-informed approach to course evaluation and course development at Umeå University.

LEQ examines prerequisites for student learning by focusing on empirically grounded learning factors that research has shown to support learning in higher education.

The guide supports teachers in analysing the learning environment of a course and using this analysis as a basis for informed course development. The appendices explain the theoretical structure of LEQ and include forms for course analysis and course reflection.

Why this matters: LEQ directs analytical attention towards conditions for learning rather than satisfaction or performance alone, supporting development work grounded in research.
How to read this resource: Start with the overall process, then explore the questionnaire and polar diagram as analytical tools. Practical instructions and forms support implementation and collegial discussion.

The LEQ Process

The LEQ process is intended as a recurring cycle of experience, analysis, and development rather than a one-off administrative activity.

The LEQ process is intended as a recurring cycle of experience, analysis, and development rather than a one-off administrative activity.

1. New course offering
A course is completed. Formative evaluation during the course may already inform adjustments.
Purpose: Provides the concrete teaching and learning experience that initiates the LEQ cycle.
Connection: Leads to systematic data collection through the LEQ survey.
2. Course survey using LEQ
Students respond to the LEQ questionnaire at the end of the course.
Purpose: Collects structured data on the learning environment.
Connection: Forms the basis for reflection and analysis.
3. Students’ course reflection (optional)
A group of students jointly interprets survey results and communicates reflections to the teacher.
Purpose: Adds a collective student perspective that supports interpretation.
Connection: Informs the teacher’s course analysis.
4. Course analysis
The teacher drafts a course analysis, discusses it collegially, and finalises conclusions.
Purpose: Interprets results and identifies priorities for development.
Connection: May reveal shared competence needs.
5. Competence development
Identified needs may lead to collective competence development activities.
Purpose: Strengthens teaching practice beyond the individual course.
Connection: Supports informed course development.
6. Course development
Planned changes are implemented, often in the next course offering.
Purpose: Tests development ideas in practice.
Connection: Feeds into the next LEQ cycle.

The Questionnaire

The LEQ questionnaire is structured around 15 learning factors, operationalised through 23 statements. Statements 1–22 address the learning environment and are grouped into three overarching dimensions.

How to read this section: Learning factors express research-based conditions for learning. The statements make these conditions observable through students’ experiences.

Meaningfulness (statements 1–7)

a) Meaningful content
Statements: 1
Description: Learning is supported when course content is perceived as interesting, engaging, or important.
b) Active trial
Statements: 2
Description: Opportunities to try ideas and learn from experience support deeper learning.
c) Challenge and support
Statements: 3, 22
Description: Learning environments that combine intellectual challenge with adequate support promote engagement.
d) Belonging and autonomy
Statements: 4, 5
Description: A sense of belonging and trust in students’ capacity to learn supports motivation.
e) Collaboration and peer learning
Statements: 6, 21
Description: Learning with others facing similar challenges supports understanding.
f) Practice and feedback
Statements: 7, 15
Description: Opportunities to practise and receive feedback before assessment support learning.

Course Analysis Meeting

The course analysis meeting supports structured collegial dialogue based on shared analytical material.

The course analysis meeting supports structured collegial dialogue based on shared analytical material.

Presentation of each course’s pedagogical structure and context.
2. Learning environment

3. Course development
Exploration of possible development directions and challenges.
4. Final reflection

Form for Course Analysis

This form supports systematic documentation of survey results and analytical conclusions.

  • Context and outcomes: Describes course conditions, response rate, and outcomes.
  • Learning environment: Interprets statements 1–22 and students’ own effort.
  • Student perspectives: Synthesises responses to open questions.
  • Development priorities: Identifies short- and long-term development intentions.
