Purpose

Systematic Course Evaluation and Development

This digital learning resource is designed to assist teaching staff at Umeå University in conducting systematic, collegial, and scientifically grounded course evaluations and developments. The core instrument is the Learning Experience Questionnaire (LEQ).

Crucially, the LEQ examines the prerequisites for student learning based on empirically grounded learning factors. It represents a shift away from evaluating teaching performance directly, focusing instead on the conditions that support higher education learning outcomes.

This guide supports continuous course evaluation and development. It operationalises the theoretical structure of the LEQ and provides structured methodologies, including forms for course analysis and course reflection, to ensure development work is founded on a scientific basis.

Theoretical Framework

The Questionnaire and Learning Factors

The LEQ's theoretical construction rests on Aaron Antonovsky's Sense of Coherence theory. The learning factors are grouped into three primary dimensions: Meaningfulness, Comprehensibility, and Manageability. Click a card to understand why each factor matters for the learning environment.

Meaningfulness (1-7) Comprehensibility (8-16) Manageability (17-22)
Meaningful content (a)

We try to answer questions or solve problems that we find interesting or important.

Statement 1: The course content felt meaningful to me as a person.
Why this matters

When students perceive content as personally or professionally relevant, intrinsic motivation increases, which promotes deeper approaches to learning.

Active trial (b)

We are able to speculate, test ideas and learn from experience.

Statement 2: I was able to try out my own ideas about the subject, theoretically or practically, and learn from experience.
Why this matters

Experiential learning requires space for active experimentation before learners possess complete knowledge, allowing them to construct understanding inductively.

Challenge (c)

We are able to do so in a challenging and supportive environment.

Statement 3: The course felt challenging in a stimulating way.
Why this matters

Appropriate cognitive friction stimulates engagement. A challenging environment must be balanced with sufficient support to prevent anxiety.

Belonging and autonomy (d)

We feel part of a community and believe others have confidence in our learning.

Statements 4 & 5: I felt a sense of community. / I felt that the teachers had confidence in my ability.
Why this matters

Socio-cultural learning theory posits that learning is fundamentally social. Feeling trusted and belonging to a community lowers affective filters.

Peer learning (e)

We collaborate with and learn from other learners.

Statement 6: Collaborating with others on the course helped me to learn.
Why this matters

Collaborating with peers who are struggling with similar conceptual thresholds often provides more accessible scaffolding than expert explanations.

Practice (f)

We are able to try, fail, and receive feedback before summative assessment.

Statement 7: During the course I was given opportunities to practice what I was expected to learn.
Why this matters

Formative practice separates the learning process from the anxiety of high-stakes grading, encouraging risk-taking and genuine skill development.

Clear organization (g)

We understand how the environment is organized and what is expected of us.

Statement 8: The course was organized in a way that helped me understand what I should do.
Why this matters

A transparent structure reduces cognitive load related to navigation and administration, allowing students to focus cognitive resources on the subject matter.

Clear communication (h)

We have sufficient prior knowledge to handle a well-adapted learning situation.

Statement 9: The course content was communicated in ways I could understand.
Why this matters

Teaching must align with the students' current zone of proximal development. Communication that bridges existing knowledge and new concepts is crucial.

Constructive alignment (i, j)

We understand expected learning outcomes and fair assessment strategies.

Statements 10, 11 & 12: Outcomes helped me understand what to learn. / Activities helped me reach outcomes. / Assessment was clear and equitable.
Why this matters

Constructive alignment ensures that the intended learning outcomes, teaching activities, and assessment methods are coherently linked, guiding student effort.

Concrete examples (k)

We are able to learn inductively from concrete examples.

Statement 13: I was able to learn from concrete examples that I could relate to.
Why this matters

Moving from concrete experiences to abstract, general principles helps students anchor theoretical concepts in relatable, real-world contexts.

Key concepts (l)

We are challenged to develop a thorough understanding of key concepts.

Statement 14: The course activities helped me develop a thorough understanding of key concepts.
Why this matters

Prioritising deep understanding of threshold concepts allows learners to gradually build a coherent whole, rather than memorising fragmented information.

Feedback & Reflection (f, m)

We receive regular feedback and opportunities to reflect.

Statements 15 & 16: I received regular feedback to see my progress. / Opportunities to reflect on what I learned.
Why this matters

Feedback directs future learning. Reflection allows students to integrate new experiences in light of previous knowledge, completing the experiential learning cycle.

Sufficient prior knowledge (h)

Our background knowledge and abilities were sufficient.

Statement 17: My background knowledge and abilities were sufficient to follow the course.
Why this matters

If the gap between expected and actual prior knowledge is too large, manageability collapses, leading to surface learning or student withdrawal.

Enough time (n)

We have enough time to learn and devote the time needed.

Statement 18: The pace of the course was reasonable in relation to what I was expected to do.
Why this matters

Deep approaches to learning require time. Excessive workload inevitably forces students into strategic, surface-level memorisation strategies.

Variation and participation (o)

We learn in different ways and influence our learning situation.

Statements 19 & 20: Enabled me to learn in different ways. / Teachers took into account my experiences.
Why this matters

Offering varied instructional methods caters to diverse student backgrounds. Allowing students to influence their situation increases a sense of manageability and agency.

Collaboration & Support (e, c)

Opportunities to collaborate and receive support.

Statements 21 & 22: Opportunities to collaborate and discuss the subject. / Able to get support from teachers or peers.
Why this matters

A supportive academic infrastructure ensures that when challenges become overwhelming, resources exist to guide the student back to productive learning.

Polar Diagram Explainer

The polar diagram visualises the average student response to statements 1 to 22. Statement 23 (own effort) is evaluated separately as its own subscale.

Interpret with care
How to interpret the diagram:
  • Points further from the centre indicate that more students considered the statement to be true.
  • A larger circular shape suggests a more beneficial learning environment in terms of the underlying learning factors.
  • Crucially: Weaker aspects are not automatically problems to fix. The diagram is a support for pedagogical analysis, not a normative judgement of teaching performance. Context dictates action.
In Practice

Practical Instructions

Guidelines and tools for executing the phases of the LEQ process efficiently.

Course Survey
Tools used: Canvas

At the end of the course, carry out a summative survey using the ready-made LEQ template in Canvas.

  • Inform students at the start of the course how course evaluation will be conducted and why it is important.
  • Report the results of the previous course evaluation, focusing on changes made.
  • Avoid inserting additional questions to the LEQ to maintain its validity.
  • Carry out the survey after the last examination, as the examination is a vital part of the learning environment.
  • At least a one-week response period is recommended, reminding students twice a week.
Course Reflection (Optional)
Tools used: Survey&Report

Students meet independently to jointly interpret survey results and pass aggregated feedback to the teacher.

  • Use the web form support resource in Survey&Report.
  • Present this possibility in connection with the start of the course.
  • A suitable size for the student group is 4 to 6 people.
  • Students should carry out the course reflection on their own, without the teacher present, to ensure open discussion.
Course Analysis
Tools used: Survey&Report

A structured preliminary analysis of the collected data, formulating conclusions about aspects to develop.

  • Use the web form in Survey&Report containing guiding questions.
  • Consider written comments heavily. For open questions, qualitative content analysis is advantageous to identify dominant themes.
  • Make the final version available to the students in accordance with the Higher Education Ordinance (Ch. 1 § 14).
Collegial Exchange

Course Analysis Meeting

The collegial meeting allows teachers to exchange concrete teaching experiences. It builds a community of practice using the preliminary course analysis as a foundation.

Key Conditions:
  • Different courses are usually preferable to broaden perspectives.
  • Participants must prepare a preliminary course analysis beforehand.
  • The chairperson facilitates rather than presents, managing time fairly.
  • The purpose is the collegial exchange of concrete teaching experiences, not 'solving problems' for one another.
  • LEQ remains an aid to analysis, not a normative judgement tool.
1

Presentation of the pedagogical structure

Briefly describe the design and implementation of the teaching for the course. Provides essential context for colleagues.

2

Stronger and weaker aspects of the learning environment

Highlight findings deduced from the polar diagram, students' written comments, and the open questions.

3

Discussion of possible course development

Discuss the respective learning environments in a freer form. Explore pedagogical adjustments based on the identified aspects.

4

Final reflection

Reflect collaboratively on the implementation and results of the meeting itself to continuously improve the collegial process.

Documentation

Course Analysis Forms

The appendices provide structured forms to guide analysis and reflection. They move users methodically from raw data to pedagogical action.

Form for Course Analysis

Used by the teacher to document findings step-by-step.

1. Background & Context

Includes: Course details, Quality perspectives (e.g., Sustainability, Gender equality), Implemented changes, Outcome data.

Why this matters

Establishes the variables that might have influenced the current learning environment.

2. Student Perspective

Includes: Response rate, Analysis of learning environment (statements 1-22), Own effort (23), Open questions analysis.

Why this matters

Forces a qualitative synthesis of quantitative LEQ data and written comments to identify dominant themes.

3. Teacher Perspective & Action

Includes: Prioritised course development, Revision of syllabus.

Why this matters

Translates the analysis into concrete, prioritised pedagogical developments for the next offering.

Form for Course Reflection

Used by the optional student group to independently clarify survey results.

1. Setup & Specifics

Includes: Course details, Specific questions conveyed by teachers.

Why this matters

Allows teachers to direct student reflection towards specific pedagogical interventions they are unsure about.

2. Collaborative Analysis

Includes: Discussion of statements 1-23, Discussion of open questions.

Why this matters

Helps teachers interpret ambiguous survey responses by providing aggregated, negotiated student consensus.

3. Actionable Suggestions

Includes: Suggestions for course development.

Why this matters

Empowers students to participate as partners in pedagogical development, rather than merely consumers.
