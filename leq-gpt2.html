Overview

This guide explains how teachers at Umeå University can work in a systematic, collegial and scientific way with course evaluation and course development. For this purpose, the Learning Experience Questionnaire, LEQ, is used as a survey tool that examines the prerequisites for students’ learning based on factors that empirical research has found to promote learning in higher education.

The first part of the guide presents the working method and the survey tool. Practical instructions are then provided. In the appendices, the theoretical structure of LEQ is explained in more detail, and two forms are included to facilitate course analysis work.

Why this mattersLEQ is not only a questionnaire. In this guide it is part of a wider process for course evaluation, course analysis, collegial exchange and course development.

What this resource helps you do

This page is designed to help you understand what LEQ is, how the process works, what the questionnaire examines, how the polar diagram can be interpreted, and how the forms support practical course development.

The LEQ Process

The so-called LEQ-process consists of six steps. It begins on completion of a course and continues into the next course offering. If the process is carried out in an active, meaningful and sustainable way over time, it can promote a community of practice, improve students’ learning, help identify competence development needs, increase the meaningfulness of course evaluation work, support more relevant student feedback, promote student participation, and ensure that development work is founded on a scientific basis.

Interpret with careThe LEQ process is best understood as an ongoing development cycle rather than a one-off administrative routine.

The Questionnaire

LEQ evaluates students’ experience of the course’s learning environment based on 15 factors for learning. The statements can be related to three dimensions of sense of coherence: meaningfulness, comprehensibility and manageability. Open a learning factor to see its description, corresponding statement numbers and related statement text.

In practiceThe questionnaire is not normative in terms of the form of teaching. The important thing is not whether teaching is based on lectures or seminars, but whether and how activities promote students’ learning.

Meaningfulness, statements 1–7

a
Meaningful content

We tend to learn most effectively when we try to answer questions, solve problems or develop skills that we find interesting, exciting or important.

Statement 1: The course content felt meaningful to me as a person.

Why this matters: Meaningfulness helps students experience the course as worth engaging with.

b
Active trial

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to speculate, test ideas, intellectually or practically, and learn from experience, even before we know much about the subject.

Statement 2: I was able to try out my own ideas about the subject, theoretically or practically, and learn from experience.

Why this matters: Trying ideas out can help students learn from experience rather than only receive information.

c
Challenge

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to do so in a challenging and at the same time supportive environment.

Statement 3: The course felt challenging in a stimulating way.

Why this matters: A stimulating level of challenge can make learning more engaging and substantial.

d
Belonging and autonomy

We tend to learn most effectively when we feel that we are part of a community and believe that other people have confidence in our ability to learn.

Statements 4–5: I felt a sense of community with others on the course. I felt that the teachers in the course had confidence in my ability to learn.

Why this matters: Community and trust can support students’ willingness to participate and persist.

e
Peer learning

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to collaborate with and learn from other learners struggling with the same problems.

Statement 6: Collaborating with others on the course helped me to learn.

Why this matters: Peer learning can make understanding visible and shared.

f
Practice

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to try, fail, and receive feedback before, and separate from, each summative assessment of our efforts.

Statement 7: During the course I was given opportunities to practice what I was expected to learn.

Why this matters: Practice before summative assessment can support development rather than only performance.

Comprehensibility, statements 8–16

g
Clear organization

We tend to learn most effectively when we understand how the environment is organized and what is expected of us.

Statement 8: The course was organized in a way that helped me understand what I should do.

Why this matters: Clear organization can reduce unnecessary uncertainty.

h
Clear communication and sufficient prior knowledge

We tend to learn most effectively when we have sufficient prior knowledge to handle our learning situation, which is at the same time well adapted to the intended level.

Statements 9 and 17: The course content was communicated in ways I could understand. My background knowledge and abilities were sufficient to follow the course.

Why this matters: Communication and the match with prior knowledge affect whether students can make sense of the course.

i
Constructive alignment

We tend to learn most effectively when we understand the meaning of the expected learning outcomes and believe that the work we are expected to do will help us achieve these.

Statements 10–11: The expected learning outcomes of the course helped me understand what I was going to learn. The course activities helped me reach the expected learning outcomes.

Why this matters: Students need to see how intended learning and course activities belong together.

j
Honesty and fairness in assessment

We tend to learn most effectively when we believe that our work will be considered in a fair and honest way.

Statement 12: My knowledge and abilities were assessed in a clear and equitable way.

Why this matters: A clear and equitable assessment environment supports trust in the course.

k
Concrete examples

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to learn inductively by moving from concrete examples and experiences to general principles, rather than the reverse.

Statement 13: I was able to learn from concrete examples that I could relate to.

Why this matters: Concrete examples can make abstract content more understandable.

l
Key concepts

We tend to learn most effectively when we are challenged to develop a thorough understanding of key concepts and gradually create a coherent whole from the content.

Statement 14: The course activities helped me develop a thorough understanding of key concepts.

Why this matters: Understanding key concepts supports coherence across the course.

f
Feedback

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to try, fail, and receive feedback before, and separate from, each summative assessment of our efforts.

Statement 15: I received regular feedback from teachers or peers that helped me see my progress.

Why this matters: Feedback helps students understand their progress and next steps.

m
Reflective observation

We tend to learn most effectively when we can reflect on new experiences in the light of previous knowledge and experience.

Statement 16: The course activities provided opportunities to reflect on what I learned.

Why this matters: Reflection can help students consolidate and interpret learning experiences.

Manageability, statements 17–22

h
Sufficient prior knowledge

We tend to learn most effectively when we have sufficient prior knowledge to handle our learning situation, which is at the same time well adapted to the intended level.

Statement 17: My background knowledge and abilities were sufficient to follow the course.

Why this matters: Manageability depends partly on whether the course is possible to follow from where students start.

n
Enough time

We tend to learn most effectively when we have enough time to learn and devote the time needed to do so.

Statement 18: The pace of the course was reasonable in relation to what I was expected to do.

Why this matters: Pace affects whether expectations feel workable.

o
Variation and participation

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to learn in different ways and have opportunities to influence our learning situation.

Statements 19–20: The course activities enabled me to learn in different ways. I felt that the teachers were interested in and took into account my experiences of the course.

Why this matters: Variation and participation can make the learning environment more workable for different students.

e
Collaboration

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to collaborate with and learn from other learners struggling with the same problems.

Statement 21: The course activities provided opportunities to collaborate with others and discuss the subject.

Why this matters: Collaboration can also make the learning situation more manageable.

c
Support

We tend to learn most effectively when we are able to do so in a challenging and at the same time supportive environment.

Statement 22: I was able to get support from teachers or peers if I needed it.

Why this matters: Support affects whether challenge remains possible to handle.

Practical Instructions

UPL and ITS have developed support resources for the LEQ process in Canvas, the Survey&Report web service and the video portal UmU Play. Open the sections below to see purpose, available tools and practical advice from the guide.

Purpose: Carry out a summative course survey using the ready-made LEQ template in Canvas.

Support resources: Canvas, UmU Play.

  • Inform students at the start of the course how course evaluation will be conducted and why it is important.
  • At the start of the course, report the results of the previous course evaluation, with a focus on the changes made.
  • Avoid inserting additional questions to the LEQ, but supplement with formative evaluations during the course if needed.
  • As a rule, carry out the course survey after the last examination, since the examination is part of the course.
  • If there have been both teachers and supervisors in the course, inform students how they should approach this when they answer the survey.
  • At least a one-week response period is recommended, with reminders about twice a week to increase response frequency.
  • To increase response rate, it may be advantageous to carry out the course survey during the last teaching session.
  • In some cases, it may also be possible to carry out the course survey in connection with the introductory meeting of a subsequent course.
  • User support can be found in UmU Play by searching for “LEQ”.

Purpose: A group of students can interpret and clarify the results of the course survey and pass these reflections on to the teacher.

Support resources: Survey&Report, UmU Play.

  • If you wish students to carry out a course reflection, present this possibility in connection with the start of the course.
  • You can ask whether students would like to organise a course reflection meeting, where a suitable group size would be 4–6 people.
  • If you have specific questions or requests for the students completing the course reflection, you can convey these using the web form.
  • As a rule, students should carry out the course reflection on their own, without the teacher being present at the meeting.
  • The more information students can access the better, provided the material does not contain offensive comments or similar.

Purpose: Analyse and document the results of the course survey using a web form with headings and guiding questions.

Support resources: Survey&Report, UmU Play.

  • Under “Response rate”, present the actual response rate to the questionnaire, based on the actual number of students who took the course.
  • Under “Analysis of the learning environment”, consider the written comments in the latter part of the course survey report.
  • For answers to the open questions, a qualitative content analysis can be used to identify and highlight themes that dominate the general picture.
  • At the same time, be aware that there may be ‘gold nuggets’ in individual comments and responses.
  • In accordance with the Higher Education Ordinance, ch.1 § 14, the final version of the course analysis must be made available to the students.

Purpose: Exchange concrete experiences regarding teaching, stimulate cooperation regarding course development, and create a collegial context for a community of practice.

Support resources: Preliminary course analyses, course survey reports, and where relevant, course reflection feedback.

  • Courses presented at the meeting should as a rule be different from each other to increase possibilities for meaningful exchange.
  • Participants should complete a preliminary course analysis before coming to the meeting.
  • The chairperson should explain the purpose of the meeting and ensure its implementation, but should not present their own course.
  • The purpose is collegial exchange of concrete experience, not that others solve all problems brought to the meeting.
  • The chairperson should remind participants that LEQ is an aid to the process and not a normative assessment of what should be developed.
  • As a rule, no notes are taken during a course analysis meeting, but participants are responsible for documenting things they want to address later.

Course Analysis Meeting Agenda

The meeting is proposed to be conducted in the form of three rounds with a final reflection. Select a round to see its purpose.

Round 1

The first round gives participants a brief account of the pedagogical structure of the course. To keep the meeting effective, everyone should be brief and general discussions should be avoided in the first rounds, although questions can of course be asked.

Course Analysis Forms

The appendices include one form for course analysis and one form for course reflection. The guide explains the content of these forms and how they help teachers and student groups interpret results, document conclusions and support beneficial course development.

Form for Course Analysis

This form helps the teacher analyse the course survey, document conclusions and identify prioritised course development.

Course information and context

  • Course name, course code, start date, end date
  • Course coordinator name and e-mail
  • Course analysis completed date

Relation to the university’s quality system

  • Research connection
  • Internationalization
  • Gender equality
  • Collaboration with society and connection with working life
  • Sustainable development
  • Student participation and student-centered learning

Why this matters: This section asks the teacher to mark perspectives that permeate the course and motivate their position in a comment.

What changed and what happened

  • Implemented changes since the last course offering
  • Outcome for course participants
  • Response rate

Analysis of results

  • Analysis of the learning environment, statements 1–22
  • Own effort, statement 23
  • Most important learning, question 24
  • Best aspects of the course, question 25
  • Suggestions for improvement, question 26
  • Good advice, question 27
  • Remaining viewpoints, question 28

In practice: These sections help the teacher move from response patterns and comments to a more nuanced interpretation of the course.

Forward-looking development

  • Prioritized course development
  • Revision of syllabus
  • Comments on the course analysis

Why this matters: The form ends by linking analysis to concrete short-term or long-term development work.

Form for Course Reflection

This form helps a student group interpret and clarify the results of the course survey and pass this on to the teachers.

Purpose and practical set-up

  • The purpose of the meeting is to help teachers interpret and clarify the results of students’ feedback in the course survey.
  • The course reflection can facilitate the teachers’ course analysis and contribute to course development in a beneficial way.
  • Teachers automatically get access to the reflection when the form has been sent.
  • The course coordinator should be notified when the course reflection is completed.

Basic meeting information

  • Specific questions from the teachers
  • Course name
  • Course coordinator name
  • Date of the meeting

Interpreting the survey results

  • Learning environment, statements 1–23
  • Open questions, questions 24–28
  • Specific questions

Interpret with care: The form invites the student group to discuss what seems particularly important or interesting to highlight, and why.

Suggestions and final comments

  • Suggestions for course development
  • Comments on the course reflection

Why this matters: This helps student feedback become clearer and more usable in the next step of course analysis.
