Course Evaluation and Development
on a Scientific Basis
using the Learning Experience Questionnaire

A guide for teachers at Umeå University on how to work in a systematic, collegial, and scientific way with course evaluation and course development — using the Learning Experience Questionnaire (LEQ).

What is the LEQ guide?

This guide introduces the Learning Experience Questionnaire and explains how to use it as part of an ongoing course development cycle.

🎯

Purpose

LEQ examines the prerequisites for students' learning based on empirically grounded learning factors — not teacher performance or student satisfaction.

🔬

Scientific basis

The questionnaire is grounded in Antonovsky's Sense of Coherence theory, Kolb's experiential learning, Vygotsky's socio-cultural theory, and Boyer's Scholarship of Teaching and Learning.

🤝

Collegial practice

The process is designed to foster a community of practice among teachers through shared analysis, collegial meetings, and mutual exchange of concrete teaching experience.

🔄

Ongoing cycle

The LEQ process is not an administrative routine. It is an ongoing cycle of survey, reflection, analysis, and development — each iteration building on the last.

Why this matters Standard course surveys often focus on teacher performance or student satisfaction, and rarely provide information that can be translated into course development that promotes learning. LEQ is designed to give teachers actionable insight grounded in research on learning.

What does the guide contain?

Overview

An introduction to the working method and the LEQ survey tool.

Practical instructions

Step-by-step guidance for carrying out the course survey, course reflection, course analysis, and course analysis meeting.

Appendix A — Theoretical design

A detailed explanation of the 15 learning factors the questionnaire is designed to examine, and the full text of all 23 statements and 5 open questions.

Appendices B & C — Forms

The LEQ Process

The LEQ process is a six-step development cycle. It is not a linear checklist — each course offering feeds into the next through analysis and reflection. Click any step to explore its purpose and connection to the cycle.

In practice The process works best when engaged with actively and sustainably over time. It can promote a community of practice, increase the meaningfulness of evaluation work, and help ensure that course development is founded on a scientific basis.
1
New Course Offering Cycle start
The process begins as the course ends, feeding insights into the next offering.

What happens

The process begins on completion of your course. During the course it is advantageous to use formative evaluation to adapt implementation to your students' needs as far as possible. Formative evaluations can also be used to evaluate individual sections of a course, which may be particularly important for longer courses.

Connection to the cycle

The new course offering is both the end point of one cycle and the starting point of the next. Planned course development from Step 6 is implemented here.

▾
2
Course Survey using LEQ Survey
A summative survey using the LEQ tool, carried out after the last examination.

What happens

At the end of the course, you carry out a summative course survey using the LEQ survey tool. Other sources of information — such as your own experiences as a teacher — can also be taken into account in the course evaluation.

Purpose

The survey generates data on how students experienced the course's learning environment across 22 statements grouped into three dimensions, plus 5 open questions. This becomes the basis for course analysis.

Tool

A ready-made LEQ template is available in the learning platform Canvas. Instructional videos are available on UmU Play (search "LEQ").

▾
3
Students' Course Reflection Optional
A student group meets independently to interpret and clarify the survey results.

What happens

You can share the results of the course survey with your students. A group of students — ideally 4–6 people — can then meet to carry out a course reflection, interpreting and clarifying the results and passing their views on to you.

Purpose

The course reflection helps you understand what lies behind the survey responses. Sometimes results are difficult to interpret without student input. The students carry out the reflection on their own, without the teacher present.

Tool

A simplified course analysis form (Appendix C) is available as a template in Survey&Report. Instructions are available on UmU Play.

▾
4
Course Analysis Draft → Meeting → Final
Preliminary analysis, followed by a collegial meeting, then a confirmed final version.

What happens

Based on the data collected, you prepare a preliminary course analysis. This is then presented and discussed at a course analysis meeting with colleagues. After the meeting, a final version is confirmed and published.

Purpose

The analysis helps you identify stronger and weaker aspects of the course's learning environment and formulate proposals for course development. The collegial meeting is central to the process — it promotes a community of practice and allows for an exchange of concrete teaching experience.

Tool

An electronic course analysis form (Appendix B) is available as a template in Survey&Report. Instructions are available on UmU Play.

▾
5
Competence Development If needed
If the analysis meeting identifies shared development needs, follow-up for teachers.

What happens

If a common need for competence development becomes apparent during the course analysis meeting, this can be followed up with some form of competence development activity for the teachers involved.

Purpose

This step ensures that systematic patterns identified across multiple courses can be addressed at the level of teacher skills and knowledge — not just individual course design.

▾
6
Course Development Development
Implementing the planned changes from the final course analysis.

What happens

You carry out the proposals for course development indicated in the final version of the course analysis. In practice, you will most likely do some of this work during the next course offering.

Connection to the cycle

Course development feeds directly into the next course offering — Step 1 — completing the cycle. Whether your development work promotes learning or not will be seen in your students' results over time.

▾
Interpret with care Step 3 (Students' Course Reflection) is optional and shown as a dashed loop in the original diagram. The process can work without it, but including students often provides valuable context for understanding what lies behind the survey responses.

The Questionnaire

LEQ is built on 15 learning factors grounded in research on teaching and learning in higher education. Each factor corresponds to one or more statements in the questionnaire. The statements are grouped into three dimensions from Antonovsky's Sense of Coherence theory.

Why this matters The questionnaire is not normative about the form of teaching. Whether a course uses lectures or seminars matters less than whether those activities promote student learning. LEQ measures prerequisites for learning — not teacher methods.
Meaningfulness

The emotional level. Statements 1–7. Do students find the course meaningful, challenging, and belonging to a learning community?

Comprehensibility

The cognitive level. Statements 8–16. Do students understand the course structure, content, and expectations?

Manageability

The instrumental level. Statements 17–22. Do students have the resources, time, and support they need?

a
Meaningful Content
Meaningfulness
We learn most effectively when we try to answer questions or solve problems we find interesting, exciting, or important.
LEQ Statement
1The course content felt meaningful to me as a person.
Why it matters: Students who find the course content personally meaningful are more motivated to engage deeply with it. Superficial relevance is not enough — the content should connect to questions they genuinely care about.
b
Active Trial
Meaningfulness
We learn when we can speculate, test ideas theoretically or practically, and learn from experience — even before knowing much about the subject.
LEQ Statement
2I was able to try out my own ideas about the subject — theoretically or practically — and learn from experience.
Why it matters: Active trial promotes deeper processing and ownership of learning. It is linked to Kolb's experiential learning model and is a prerequisite for the kind of learning that has a lasting effect.
c
Challenge and Support
Meaningfulness + Manageability
We learn in environments that are challenging in a stimulating way and, at the same time, supportive.
LEQ Statements
3The course felt challenging in a stimulating way.
22I was able to get support from teachers or peers if I needed it.
Why it matters: Challenge without support creates anxiety; support without challenge creates complacency. Both are needed for productive learning. Note that statement 22 appears in the Manageability dimension.
d
Belonging and Autonomy
Meaningfulness
We learn when we feel part of a community and believe that others have confidence in our ability to learn.
LEQ Statements
4I felt a sense of community with others on the course.
5I felt that the teachers in the course had confidence in my ability to learn.
Why it matters: A sense of belonging and trust is closely linked to student motivation and persistence. It is grounded in socio-cultural learning theory — we develop more effectively when we feel seen and trusted.
e
Collaboration and Peer Learning
Meaningfulness + Manageability
We learn when we are able to collaborate with and learn from other learners struggling with the same problems.
LEQ Statements
6Collaborating with others on the course helped me to learn.
21The course activities provided opportunities to collaborate with others and discuss the subject.
Why it matters: Collaboration is not just a pedagogical method — it is a condition for learning. Statement 6 (Meaningfulness) captures the experience of learning through collaboration; statement 21 (Manageability) captures whether opportunities existed.
f
Practice and Feedback
Meaningfulness + Comprehensibility
We learn when we can try, fail, and receive feedback before and separately from each summative assessment.
LEQ Statements
7During the course I was given opportunities to practice what I was expected to learn.
15I received regular feedback from teachers or peers that helped me see my progress.
Why it matters: Practice and feedback are essential prerequisites for deep learning. Statement 7 (Meaningfulness) measures practice opportunities; statement 15 (Comprehensibility) measures the quality of feedback received.
g
Clear Organisation
Comprehensibility
We learn when we understand how the environment is organised and what is expected of us.
LEQ Statement
8The course was organised in a way that helped me understand what I should do.
Why it matters: When students are unclear about what is expected of them, cognitive resources are spent managing uncertainty rather than learning. Clear organisation reduces this cognitive load.
h
Sufficient Prior Knowledge and Clear Communication
Comprehensibility + Manageability
We learn when we have sufficient prior knowledge and the course is well adapted to the intended level.
LEQ Statements
9The course content was communicated in ways I could understand.
17My background knowledge and abilities were sufficient to follow the course.
Why it matters: Statement 9 (Comprehensibility) reflects whether the teaching was pitched at the right level; statement 17 (Manageability) reflects whether students had the prerequisites. Together they help identify a mismatch between expected and actual prior knowledge.
i
Constructive Alignment
Comprehensibility
We learn when we understand the expected learning outcomes and believe that the work we are expected to do will help us achieve them.
LEQ Statements
10The expected learning outcomes of the course helped me understand what I was going to learn.
11The course activities helped me reach the expected learning outcomes.
Why it matters: Constructive alignment — the coherence between learning outcomes, teaching activities, and assessment — is a foundational principle of effective course design. When students perceive this alignment, they can direct their learning effort more effectively.
j
Honesty and Fairness
Comprehensibility
We learn when we believe that our work will be considered in a fair and honest way.
LEQ Statement
12My knowledge and abilities were assessed in a clear and equitable way.
Why it matters: Perceived fairness in assessment affects student trust in the course and willingness to invest effort. A sense of inequity can undermine motivation even when other aspects of the course are strong.
k
Concrete Examples
Comprehensibility
We learn inductively by moving from concrete examples and experiences to general principles, rather than the reverse.
LEQ Statement
13I was able to learn from concrete examples that I could relate to.
Why it matters: Abstract content becomes accessible when anchored in concrete examples. This reflects a well-established finding in cognitive science: learning proceeds more effectively from the specific to the general.
l
Key Concepts
Comprehensibility
We learn when we are challenged to develop a thorough understanding of key concepts and gradually create a coherent whole from the content.
LEQ Statement
14The course activities helped me develop a thorough understanding of key concepts.
Why it matters: Deep understanding of key concepts — rather than broad but shallow coverage — is a predictor of transferable learning. This factor reflects the importance of depth over breadth in course design.
m
Reflective Observation
Comprehensibility
We learn when we can reflect on new experiences in the light of previous knowledge and experience.
LEQ Statement
16The course activities provided opportunities to reflect on what I learned.
Why it matters: Reflection is a bridge between experience and understanding. It is a central element of Kolb's experiential learning cycle and is essential for turning lived experience into lasting knowledge.
n
Enough Time and Own Effort
Manageability
We learn when we have enough time to learn and devote the time needed to do so.
LEQ Statements
18The pace of the course was reasonable in relation to what I was expected to do.
23I studied to the extent I needed to comprehend the content of the course.
Why it matters: Statement 18 reflects whether the course design allowed adequate time. Statement 23 captures students' own effort and is treated as a separate subscale — it is not included in the polar diagram but is important for contextualising results.
o
Variation and Participation
Manageability
We learn when we are able to learn in different ways and have opportunities to influence our own learning situation.
LEQ Statements
19The course activities enabled me to learn in different ways.
20I felt that the teachers were interested in and took into account my experiences of the course.
Why it matters: Variation in teaching methods accommodates different learning styles and maintains engagement. Student participation — feeling that one's voice is heard — reinforces a sense of autonomy and ownership of learning.
Polar diagram The 22 statements above (statements 1–22) form the axes of the polar diagram in the LEQ report. Statement 23, on students' own effort, is shown separately — it is treated as its own subscale because psychometric evaluation showed it should not be combined with the other 22.

Polar Diagram Explainer

The LEQ report presents an overview of results as a polar (radar) diagram with 22 axes — one per statement. Hover or tap any axis number to see the full statement and its dimension.

Dimensions
Meaningfulness — statements 1–7
The emotional level
Comprehensibility — statements 8–16
The cognitive level
Manageability — statements 17–22
The instrumental level
How to interpret

Points further from centre indicate more students agreed with that statement.

A large circular shape indicates a broadly beneficial learning environment in terms of the underlying learning factors.

Weaker aspects are not automatically problems to fix. The diagram is a support for analysis, not a normative judgement. Context determines what is worth addressing.

Note Statement 23 (own effort) is not included in the polar diagram. It is treated as its own subscale based on psychometric evaluation.

Practical Instructions

Guidance for carrying out each phase of the LEQ process, including available support resources and practical tips from the guide. Open each section to explore.

📋
Course Survey
Summative survey using LEQ — carried out after the last examination
▾

The course survey is the foundation of the LEQ process. It collects students' experiences of the course's learning environment using 22 statements and 5 open questions, generating the data for your course analysis.

📋 Canvas — LEQ template🎬 UmU Play (search "LEQ")
  • Inform students at the start of the course how evaluation will be conducted and why it is important.
  • At the start of the course, report the results of the previous course evaluation, with a focus on the changes made.
  • Avoid inserting additional questions to LEQ, but feel free to supplement with formative evaluations during the course.
  • The course survey should as a rule be carried out after the last examination, as the examination is part of the course.
  • If there have been both teachers and supervisors, inform students how to approach this when answering.
  • Recommend at least a one-week response period and remind students approximately twice a week.
  • Carrying out the survey during the last teaching session can increase the response rate.
  • It may also be possible to carry out the survey during the introductory meeting of a subsequent course.
💬
Course Reflection
Optional student group meeting to interpret the survey results
▾

A group of students meets independently to review and interpret the course survey results. This helps you understand what lies behind the data and provides additional context for your course analysis. The teachers do not attend.

📊 Survey&Report — Appendix C form🎬 UmU Play
  • Present the possibility of a course reflection at the start of the course.
  • A suitable group size is 4–6 students. Ask if anyone would like to organise the meeting.
  • Convey specific questions or requests to the students via the web form if needed.
  • Students should carry out the reflection on their own, without the teacher present at the meeting.
  • Provide all relevant survey data — but remove personal attacks or offensive comments before sharing.
🔍
Course Analysis
Systematic analysis and documentation of evaluation results
▾

The course analysis is where you interpret the survey data, identify stronger and weaker aspects of the learning environment, and formulate proposals for course development. The electronic form (Appendix B) guides you through this step by step.

📊 Survey&Report — Appendix B form🎬 UmU Play
  • Under "Response rate", present the actual rate based on the number of students who took the course.
  • Under "Analysis of the learning environment", consider any written comments from the survey report carefully.
  • For the open question answers, use qualitative content analysis — identify and highlight the themes that dominate the general picture.
  • Be aware that there may be "gold nuggets" in individual comments and responses.
  • Per the Higher Education Ordinance (ch.1 § 14), the final version of the course analysis must be made available to students.
🤝
Course Analysis Meeting
Collegial exchange of concrete teaching experience with colleagues
▾

The course analysis meeting is a central part of the LEQ process. Its purpose is to offer participants the opportunity to exchange concrete experiences of teaching and to promote collegiality — not to solve problems or reach consensus, but to learn from each other.

Interpret with care The aim of the meeting is not for other participants or the chairperson to solve any problems that are brought to the meeting, even if the meeting can lead to some problems being solved. The chairperson should remind participants that LEQ is an aid to the process, not a normative judgement.
Four-round agenda
Round 1
Pedagogical structure
Round 2
Stronger and weaker aspects
Round 3
Discussion of development
Round 4
Final reflection
Round 1 — Presentation of the pedagogical structure

Each teacher briefly describes their course's pedagogical design — how it is structured, what teaching methods are used, and what the overall learning design looks like. Keep presentations concise. Questions of clarification are welcome, but general discussions should be avoided during this round.

Round 2 — Stronger and weaker aspects of the learning environment

Each teacher presents the stronger and weaker aspects identified in the polar diagram. Students' written comments and open question answers can help explain what lies behind the results. If a course reflection has been carried out, this can also be used as source material. Keep this round concise.

Interpret with careStronger and weaker aspects in the polar diagram are starting points for analysis, not evaluations. There may be good reasons why one aspect is weaker than others, and some aspects are easier to influence than others.
Round 3 — Discussion of possible course development

Each teacher's learning environment is discussed in freer form. The chairperson distributes time fairly between the courses, taking into account that some time should be reserved for the final reflection. This is the core collegial exchange of the meeting.

Round 4 — Final reflection

A collective reflection on the implementation and results of the meeting as a whole. What common educational challenges emerged? What is worth carrying forward? The chairperson may convey what emerged to the pedagogical leader of the department or programme if relevant.

Key conditions for the meeting
🔀As a rule, the courses should be different from each other to maximise exchange of experience.
📝All participants should prepare a preliminary course analysis before attending.
🎙️The chairperson facilitates rather than presents — they should not present their own course.
⏱️Each course takes roughly 30–40 minutes. Presenting 3–5 courses per meeting is appropriate.
📵No notes are taken as a rule. Each participant is responsible for documenting what they wish to follow up.
🔬After the meeting, update your course analysis and make it available to students, teachers, and affected programmes.

Course Analysis Forms

Two forms support the LEQ process. Both are available as ready-made templates in Survey&Report. The descriptions below explain the purpose of each section to help you use the forms effectively.

In practice The forms are tools to guide and document your thinking — not bureaucratic checklists. The guiding questions in each section are starting points, not requirements to be answered exhaustively. The purpose is to help you formulate and document your conclusions in a systematic way.
